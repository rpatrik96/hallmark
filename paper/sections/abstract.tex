\begin{abstract}
LLM use for literature review is prevalent, but authors, reviewers, and venues rarely verify AI-generated references---as shown by the NeurIPS 2025 incident in which 53 accepted papers carried fabricated citations.
Citation verification workflows, including LLM-assisted ones, are emerging, but there is no standardized measurement of how well they perform.
We introduce \textsc{Hallmark} (\textbf{Hall}ucination bench\textbf{mark}), a benchmark of BibTeX entries grouped into three difficulty tiers by how much external lookup they require, with a held-out contamination-resistant split and six structured per-entry checks---DOI, authors, venue, title, field completeness, cross-database agreement---so that every miss is traced to the specific check that failed.
We evaluate a DOI-lookup baseline, six zero-shot LLMs, two agentic GPT-5.1 variants with a five-call lookup budget (against either public APIs or our co-designed rule-based verifier \texttt{bibtex-updater}), and \texttt{bibtex-updater} alone.
Three findings emerge.
First, tool access is not a free lunch: giving the LLM a matched lookup budget closes the recall gap with \texttt{bibtex-updater} but inflates false positives nearly threefold, so F1 never beats the zero-shot baseline.
Second, at the per-citation base rate implied by the NeurIPS 2025 incident, the best precision across all settings is roughly one real hallucination per ten flagged citations---calibration, not detection rate, is the binding deployment constraint.
Third, on 480 papers published after every LLM's training cutoff, false positives multiply and F1 collapses to chance, showing that LLM verifiers fail on recent work for a simple reason: they have not seen it.
\textsc{Hallmark} ships with versioned splits, a baseline registry, and a pinned evaluation protocol, as an open-source, pip-installable package.
Code and data: \url{https://github.com/anonymous/hallmark}.
\end{abstract}
