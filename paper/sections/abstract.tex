\begin{abstract}
Citation hallucination---where language models generate plausible but fabricated references---poses a growing threat to scientific integrity.
The NeurIPS 2025 incident (53 accepted papers with fabricated citations) and subsequent large-scale audits underscore the urgency of automated citation metadata verification, yet no standardized benchmark exists to compare verification tools.
We introduce \textsc{Hallmark} (\textbf{Hall}ucination bench\textbf{mark}), comprising 2,525 BibTeX entries spanning 14 hallucination types organized into three difficulty tiers.
Each entry includes six independently verifiable sub-tests---DOI resolution, title existence, author matching, venue validity, field completeness, and cross-database agreement---decomposing binary detection into diagnosable failure modes.
We evaluate seven full-coverage tools (a DOI-only baseline, five open-weight LLMs, and GPT-5.1), revealing that DOI-only detects 26\% of hallucinations while LLMs achieve 48--88\%, with a pronounced recall--precision spectrum.
However, a 480-entry temporal supplement reveals that LLM false positive rates increase 3--5$\times$ on papers from 2024--2025 and F1 collapses to near-chance, establishing temporal robustness as a first-order deployment concern.
The benchmark, evaluation infrastructure, and all data are open-source and pip-installable.
Code and data: \url{https://github.com/anonymous/hallmark}.
\end{abstract}
