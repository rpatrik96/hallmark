\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Citation hallucination---the generation of plausible but fabricated bibliographic references by language models---has emerged as a concrete threat to scientific publishing.
In late 2025, the NeurIPS program committee identified 53 accepted papers containing fabricated citations that had passed peer review~\citep{neurips2025incident}.
Independent audits confirmed the scale of the problem: \citet{ghostcite2026} analyzed 2.2 million citations across 56,000 papers and flagged 604 papers with likely hallucinated references; \citet{hallucitation2026} found over 100 affected main-conference papers in ACL, NAACL, and EMNLP alone; and \citet{mysterious2026} documented a sharp rise from zero detected cases in 2021 to consistent problems across HPC venues by 2025.
These findings reveal that citation hallucination is not anecdotal but systematic, and that neither authors nor reviewers reliably catch fabricated references.

Several citation verification tools have been developed in response, ranging from DOI resolution checkers to multi-database cross-referencing systems.
However, \emph{no standardized benchmark exists to compare these tools}: each is evaluated on ad-hoc datasets under different conditions, making it impossible to assess relative strengths, failure modes, or coverage gaps.

\paragraph{Scope.}
\textsc{Hallmark} focuses on citation \emph{metadata} verification---detecting fabricated, inconsistent, or nonexistent bibliographic references---not claim-level hallucination, which requires full-text analysis and represents an orthogonal challenge.

We address this gap with four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A hallucination taxonomy} of 14 types across three difficulty tiers, from fabricated DOIs to plausible fabrications (\cref{sec:taxonomy}).
    \item \textbf{A benchmark dataset} of 2,525 BibTeX entries with six diagnostic sub-tests per entry, enabling fine-grained failure analysis inspired by HumanEval~\citep{humaneval} (\cref{sec:dataset}).
    \item \textbf{An evaluation protocol} with tier-weighted F1, calibration assessment, and Plackett-Luce ranking~\citep{luce1959,plackett1975} for incomplete data (\cref{sec:evaluation}).
    \item \textbf{Open infrastructure}: pip-installable package with baseline registry, CI pipeline, and community contribution system (\cref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

We validate the benchmark on seven full-coverage tools (\cref{sec:experiments}), revealing that DOI-only detects 26\% of hallucinations while zero-shot LLM verifiers achieve 48--88\%, with a pronounced recall--precision spectrum across models.
A 480-entry temporal supplement exposes a critical deployment concern: LLM false positive rates increase 3--5$\times$ on papers from 2024--2025, collapsing F1 to near-chance (\cref{sec:temporal_robustness}).
