\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Evaluated tools}
\label{sec:baselines}

We evaluate seven full-coverage tools on \texttt{dev\_public}: a DOI-only lower bound, GPT-5.1~\citep{openai2025gpt5} (\texttt{gpt-5.1}), and five open-weight LLMs via OpenRouter (DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeek-V3.2~\citep{deepseekv3}, Qwen3-235B~\citep{qwen3}, Mistral Large~\citep{mistral2025large}, Gemini~2.5~Flash~\citep{google2025gemini}; model IDs: \texttt{deepseek/deepseek-r1}, \texttt{deepseek/deepseek-v3.2}, \texttt{qwen/qwen3-235b-a22b-2507}, \texttt{mistralai/mistral-large-2512}, \texttt{google/gemini-2.5-flash}).
All LLM baselines use the same zero-shot prompt; DeepSeek-R1 uses chain-of-thought (${\sim}25$s/entry vs.\ ${\sim}5$s).
The codebase also registers \texttt{llm\_anthropic} (Claude Sonnet~4.5, \texttt{claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929}) and Anthropic agentic variants; these are omitted from \cref{tab:results} as they were completed after the submission deadline and are reported in \cref{app:llm-setup}.
We also attempted two dedicated tools (HaRC~\citep{harc2024}, verify-citations~\citep{verifycitations2025}), both rate-limited to $<$7\% coverage due to Semantic Scholar API constraints.
The DOI-only baseline checks whether each entry's DOI resolves via CrossRef; results include the shared pre-screening layer (\cref{sec:prescreening}).

Three key insights emerge from the independent tools.
First, \emph{LLMs dramatically outperform DOI-only}: five of six achieve ${\geq}69\%$ DR---a $3{\times}$+ improvement---demonstrating that language models have sufficient parametric knowledge to detect citation hallucinations, including types they routinely generate.
Second, \emph{LLMs span a recall--precision spectrum}: from conservative (Gemini~2.5~Flash: 48\% DR, 10\% FPR) to aggressive (DeepSeek-V3.2: 88\% DR, 73\% FPR), with GPT-5.1 achieving the best balance (F1\,=\,0.822, ECE\,=\,0.107).
Third, \emph{a capability gap remains}: even the highest-recall independent model misses 12\% of hallucinations, with systematic weaknesses on subtle types.
Fourth, \emph{granting the LLM a matched lookup budget closes the recall gap but inflates false positives}: giving GPT-5.1 up to five tool calls per entry (CrossRef, OpenAlex, arXiv, or bibtex-updater) lifts DR from 0.797 to 0.956--0.965---matching bibtex-updater's 0.946---but FPR rises from 0.171 to 0.46, because absence of evidence in any one source is interpreted as evidence of fabrication.
The BTU-only agentic variant (DR\,=\,0.965, ECE\,=\,0.113) slightly dominates the multi-source variant because BTU's pre-distilled verdict is easier to interpret than raw API responses.
Co-designed tools achieve the highest raw detection rate (bibtex-updater: DR\,=\,0.946), at the cost of possible construct-overfitting bias; their numbers should be read as an upper-bound reference rather than a fair comparison.
We emphasize DR and FPR as primary metrics since they are prevalence-independent.\footnote{F1 is prevalence-sensitive and should only be compared within the same split.}

We compute 95\% confidence intervals via stratified bootstrap (10{,}000 resamples; \cref{app:statistics}).

\subsection{Per-tier analysis}
\label{sec:per_tier}

\cref{fig:tier} shows detection rates by difficulty tier.
DOI-only detection concentrates in Tier~1; all LLMs achieve strong cross-tier performance with graceful degradation.
GPT-5.1 achieves 87\%/76\%/79\% on Tiers~1/2/3.
Aggressive models achieve near-perfect Tier~1 (97--99\%) partly reflecting high FPR rather than superior discrimination.
On Tier~3, rates range from 46\% (Gemini) to 83\% (DeepSeek-R1), confirming the recall--precision tradeoff persists across difficulty levels.

\subsection{Per-type analysis}
\label{sec:per_type}

\cref{fig:heatmap} shows the per-type detection heatmap.
GPT-5.1 achieves ${\geq}67\%$ on 9 of 11 main types, with near-perfect \texttt{nonexistent\_venue} (97\%) and \texttt{placeholder\_authors} (94\%).
Its weakest types---\texttt{author\_mismatch} (63\%) and \texttt{near\_miss\_title} (63\%)---require exact bibliographic knowledge, providing concrete improvement targets.
Per-type comparisons should be interpreted directionally: with $n \approx 30$, the MDE at 80\% power is 20--26pp (\cref{app:statistics}).
