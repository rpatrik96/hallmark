\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Evaluated tools}
\label{sec:baselines}

We evaluate three independent external citation verification tools (HaRC, verify-citations, GPT-5.1) and two open-weight LLMs accessed via OpenRouter (Qwen3-235B, DeepSeek-V3.2), plus DOI-only, an internal lower-bound baseline, on the \texttt{dev\_public} split.
We additionally evaluate \texttt{bibtex-updater}~\citep{bibtexupdater} (DR\,=\,0.946) and a \emph{tool-augmented} variant that injects bibtex-updater's structured output into GPT-5.1's prompt; both are co-developed by the authors and reported separately in \cref{app:codesign} due to co-design bias concerns.

\paragraph{DOI-only.}
A minimal baseline that checks whether each entry's DOI field resolves via the CrossRef API.
Entries with a non-resolving DOI are flagged as hallucinated; entries without a DOI field are conservatively assumed valid.
Results in \cref{tab:results} include the shared pre-screening layer (DOI format validation, year bounds, author heuristics; \cref{sec:prescreening}); without pre-screening, overall detection rate drops by ${\sim}5$pp (from 25.6\% to 20.3\%), with the largest effect on Tier~1 (${\sim}18$pp).
This baseline tests the lower bound of what simple metadata checks can achieve.

\paragraph{HaRC.}
The Hallucinated Reference Checker~\citep{harc2024} queries Semantic Scholar, DBLP, Google Scholar, and Open Library.
It uses a multi-stage pipeline: DOI lookup, title search, author verification, and venue cross-check.
Due to Semantic Scholar API rate limiting, HaRC completed evaluation on only 20 of 1,119 dev entries (1.8\%) within our timeout budget.

\paragraph{verify-citations.}
A pip-installable tool~\citep{verifycitations2025} that queries arXiv, ACL Anthology, Semantic Scholar, DBLP, Google Scholar, and DuckDuckGo.
Like HaRC, it was rate-limited and completed 71 of 1,119 entries (6.3\%).

\paragraph{Open-weight LLMs (OpenRouter).}
We evaluate two open-weight models---Qwen3-235B~\citep{qwen3} and DeepSeek-V3.2~\citep{deepseekv3}---using the same zero-shot prompt as GPT-5.1, accessed via the OpenRouter API.
These models test whether the strong LLM performance generalizes beyond proprietary frontier models.

The results reveal four key insights about the current citation verification landscape.
First, \emph{API tool coverage is the binding constraint}: DOI-only is the only API-based tool with full coverage, catching 25.6\% of hallucinations---limited by design to entries with resolvable DOIs---while multi-source tools (HaRC, verify-citations) were bottlenecked by rate limiting to 1.8\% and 6.3\% coverage respectively, preventing any conclusion about their detection quality at scale.
Second, \emph{LLMs dramatically outperform API-based tools}: all three LLMs achieve ${\geq}80\%$ detection rate---a $3{\times}$+ improvement over DOI-only---demonstrating that language models can identify the very hallucinations they produce.
Third, \emph{LLMs exhibit a recall--precision tradeoff}: open-weight models (Qwen3-235B, DeepSeek-V3.2) achieve higher detection rates (83--88\%) than GPT-5.1 (80\%), but at the cost of substantially higher false positive rates (55--73\% vs.\ 17\%).
This aggressive over-flagging degrades their F1 (0.72--0.74 vs.\ 0.82), MCC (0.19--0.31 vs.\ 0.64), and calibration (ECE 0.29--0.33 vs.\ 0.11).
GPT-5.1 remains the best-performing LLM overall, achieving the highest F1, MCC, and best-calibrated confidence estimates.
Fourth, \emph{a capability gap remains}: even DeepSeek-V3.2, the highest-recall model, misses 12\% of hallucinations, with systematic weaknesses on subtle types, providing concrete improvement targets.
Since Detection Rate and FPR are prevalence-independent, we emphasize these as primary metrics.\footnote{F1 is prevalence-sensitive: a benchmark with 50\% hallucination prevalence inflates F1 relative to real-world deployment where hallucination rates may be much lower. F1 values should only be compared within the same split, never across splits or with external results.}

\paragraph{GPT-5.1 (zero-shot).}
We evaluate GPT-5.1~\citep{openai2025gpt5} as a zero-shot citation verifier: each BibTeX entry is presented with a prompt asking the model to classify it as \texttt{VALID} or \texttt{HALLUCINATED} with a confidence score.
Unlike API-dependent tools, all LLM baselines achieve full coverage (100\% of entries).
Among the three LLMs, GPT-5.1 achieves the best balance of recall and precision: its FPR (17\%) is $3$--$4{\times}$ lower than the open-weight models, yielding the highest F1 (0.822) and best calibration (ECE\,=\,0.107).

\paragraph{Incomplete-coverage tools.}
HaRC and verify-citations completed only 1.8\% and 6.3\% of entries respectively due to API rate limiting.
Their metrics reflect performance on the completed subset only and are not directly comparable to full-coverage tools.
Plackett-Luce ranking (\cref{app:ranking}) provides a principled framework for comparing tools with heterogeneous coverage but requires substantially higher pairwise overlap than achieved here.
With current tool coverage (HaRC: 1.8\%, verify-citations: 6.3\%), pairwise overlap is insufficient for stable parameter estimates; we report mean-score ranking as the primary comparison and provide Plackett-Luce infrastructure for future denser evaluations.

We compute 95\% confidence intervals using stratified bootstrap (10{,}000 resamples, stratified by hallucination type).
With $\geq$30 instances per type, per-type metrics have narrower confidence intervals than the original 10-instance design; we provide exact counts and power analysis in \cref{app:statistics}.

\subsection{Per-tier analysis}
\label{sec:per_tier}

\cref{fig:tier} shows detection rates broken down by difficulty tier.
DOI-only detection is concentrated in Tier~1, as expected, with near-zero performance on Tiers~2 and~3.
All three LLMs achieve strong performance across tiers, with graceful degradation as difficulty increases---consistent with the tier design.
GPT-5.1 achieves 87\%/76\%/79\% on Tiers~1/2/3 respectively.
The open-weight models achieve near-perfect Tier~1 detection (Qwen3-235B: 97\%, DeepSeek-V3.2: 99\%), but this partly reflects their aggressive flagging behavior (high FPR) rather than superior discrimination.
On the harder tiers, all three LLMs perform comparably (Tier~2: 76--86\%, Tier~3: 79--83\%), suggesting that the recall--precision tradeoff primarily manifests as over-flagging easy cases.
The tier structure exposes the capability gap between API-based and LLM-based approaches: API tools lack the cross-referencing and semantic reasoning needed for Tier~2--3 hallucinations, while LLMs bring parametric knowledge that compensates for missing API coverage.

\subsection{Per-type analysis}
\label{sec:per_type}

\cref{fig:heatmap} shows the per-type detection heatmap across all evaluated tools.
With $\geq$30 instances per type, per-type breakdowns provide statistically meaningful comparisons.
DOI-only detects \texttt{fabricated\_doi} reliably but misses all types that use valid DOIs.
GPT-5.1 achieves ${\geq}67\%$ detection on 9 of 11 main types, with near-perfect detection of \texttt{nonexistent\_venue} (97\%) and \texttt{placeholder\_authors} (94\%).
Its weakest types---\texttt{author\_mismatch} (63\%, stored as \texttt{swapped\_authors} in data files for backward compatibility) and \texttt{near\_miss\_title} (63\%)---involve subtle metadata perturbations that require exact bibliographic knowledge, providing concrete targets for future work.
Per-type comparisons should be interpreted directionally: with $n \approx 30$ instances per type, the minimum detectable effect is ${\sim}25$pp, so small differences between tools on a single type are not reliable; tier-level comparisons have stronger statistical grounding.
